Enhancing Climate Literacy: ​A Collective Climate Accountability and Adaptation Project

“When the climate changes, our community changes. And if we do not change with our climate, ​our culture has the potential to be lost. ​We must accept and adapt to climate change to sustain our Dënësułinë values.”​

- Dënë Cheecham-Uhrich, ​a climate activist and forest-water champion from the Clearwater River Dënë First Nation ​in La Loche, Saskatchewan.

We are working together on an ​Environment Canada and Climate Change funded project, “Enhancing Climate Literacy: ​A Collective Climate Accountability and Adaptation Project (CCAAP)". Our focus is on Canada’s boreal forest, ​home for many Indigenous communities.​

The Dënësułinë people’s culture reflects their intricate knowledge of the relationships among the forest, the water and their people.​ As land and water changed, they observed and adapted to these changes. ​The boreal landscape has undergone substantial natural resource extraction activities, making Indigenous communities concerned for their health and well-being. Multiple stressors associated with climate change ​are fundamentally changing the forest-water nexus.​

Temperatures are increasing.​ The highest concentration of uranium for nuclear energy – one of Canada’s solutions to achieve net zero carbon emissions by 2050 – is in the western boreal forest. An increase in fire activity ​– “more area burning, more fires burning, a much longer fire season” – ​is accelerating the changes to the boreal forest. ​(Lynn Johnston, forest fire research specialist at the Canadian Forest Service). Browning and falling trees are the direct result of climate change​ (Barry Cooke, research specialist at the Canadian Forest Service).

"The traditional way of life of indigenous peoples has contributed little to climate change, yet they are the most adversely affected by it."

- Gita Cosentino, Indigenous Peoples Advisory Consultant, World Bank.

The Canadian government entered into treaties with Indigenous peoples.​ However, the Indigenous people were ​not equal partners in these treaties.​ Despite their earlier history, ​some Indigenous leaders want to contribute as equal partners to Canada’s responsibility to lead on climate action.​

Our goal is to create a new generation of climate leaders.​

PROJECT PHASES

1) Climate Literacy​

We are exploring if there is a common language between Indigenous knowledge and conventional science, or if can be integrated into unrealized knowledge.​

2) Climate Science

We are working to advance our understanding of the inextricable linkages of the forest-water nexus and the people that depend on them.​

3) Climate Action and Adaptation

We are helping communities slow down the rate of climate change, while adapting to the new forest-water nexus. Managing the forest-water nexus can lead to improved community well-being.​

To protect our future, ​we must work for our future.​
